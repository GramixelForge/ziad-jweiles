About the Author

Ziad Jweiles has been obsessed with writing stories since he was five years old. For him, writing has never been about fame or fortune—it's about sharing the worlds in his imagination with readers around the globe. Each story he writes represents a piece of himself offered to the world.

His writing process is uniquely personal: all his stories begin as dreams, blended with his own life experiences. The dark fantasy realms of the Zemoria Chronicles were born from this distinctive fusion of subconscious visions and personal journeys.

When not writing, Ziad immerses himself in story-driven games and soulslikes—he's a passionate gamer who appreciates rich narratives and challenging gameplay. These interactive stories frequently inspire his own creative process.

As someone with high-functioning autism, Ziad has found that writing allows him to communicate in ways that feel authentic and meaningful. He hopes his journey can inspire others to pick up a pen (or keyboard) and share their own unique stories with the world.

The Zemoria Chronicles represents his creative heart—a series where inner demons become literal ones, and every character's struggle reflects the complexities we all face. Currently, the series includes "The Lord Under" and "The Fractured Tree," with more dark, dream-born tales in development.

Writing Philosophy

"I believe every person has a story worth telling. My dreams provide the settings—dark forests, crumbling empires, impossible choices—but it's the human experiences we all share that populate them with real emotion. I write for the quiet dreamers, the gamers who see stories everywhere, and anyone who's ever felt their inner world was too complex to explain. If my words help one person feel seen or inspire them to create, I've succeeded."

The World of Zemoria

A guide to the lore, history, and mythology of the Zemoria Chronicles

Spoiler Warning

This lore bible contains significant spoilers for The Fractured Tree and The Lord Under, as well as details about books currently in development. Sections marked with a spoiler toggle reveal information about major plot points, character fates, and story outcomes. Read at your own discretion.

Cosmology of Zemoria

The six primordial Forces. The World Tree as accidental structure. The laws that govern all of existence.

Order

Structure, pattern, law. Symbol: Interlocking Circles.

Chaos

Change, randomness, potential. Symbol: Broken Spiral.

Creation

Growth, genesis, emergence. Symbol: Blooming Flower.

Destruction

Entropy, unmaking, endings. Symbol: Shattered Stone.

Time

Sequence, causality, memory. Symbol: Interlocking Spirals.

Will

Consciousness, direction, choice. Symbol: Single Eye.

Core Principle: No single Force can manifest alone. Every event requires at least two Forces interacting. This is why magic has rules, why creation has cost, and why nothing in Zemoria exists in true isolation.

The World Tree — The Beautiful Accident

The World Tree was not planted by gods or created by design. It was born from the friction energy generated by the Six Forces clashing eternally — that friction crystallized into the First Seed. The Tree is not holy. It is not the source of life. It is a temporary solution to a permanent problem — a beautiful prison that was never meant to last forever.

  • The Conductor: Guides Forces along safe pathways, makes magic possible.
  • The Filter: Cleans chaotic Force energy into usable forms; creates seasons, weather, day and night.
  • The Memory (Songroot): Stores echoes of all that happens, which is why some places in Zemoria seem to remember emotions or events from the distant past.

The Law of Divine Power

Gods draw power from two equal sources. Remove either and a god weakens catastrophically.

  • The Forces: Their innate connection to the Force combinations that shaped them.
  • Worshippers' Belief: Exactly half their power comes from what people believe and say about them — the stories told, the prayers spoken, the names used.

A god does not choose their domain. Their domain is chosen for them by what their civilization needs them to be. Two gods with identical Force combinations become completely different beings based on who worships them and how.

Doom Debt — The Cost of Changing Fate

One cannot truly rewrite history — only rearrange its order. Healing one future breaks another. Saving one life dooms another. Every corrected event branches into a Doom Debt — something must pay the cost later. The Keepers of Songroot exist not to prevent doom, but to allocate it to the smallest possible branch.

Zemoria & Earth

Zemoria is a parallel universe to Earth. The old age of Zemoria is equivalent to Earth's stone age — prehistoric, hundreds of thousands of years before recorded human history. After a key event in the Songroot timeline, destiny slowly merges the two worlds seamlessly over centuries. By the time ancient civilizations like Egypt, Greece, and Mesoamerica are flourishing, Zemoria and Earth have become the same world. The myths we know from ancient history are the degraded oral traditions of Zemoria's real past.

Shadowflame — The Forbidden Path

Shadowflame is Destruction + Chaos magic fueled by pain, fear, and negative will. Using pain as fuel temporarily boosts Root Rank, but each boost carries a permanent cost to the user's body and mind. At its most extreme, the user becomes a Torment Engine — no longer truly a person at all. The basis of the Last Testament's teachings.

History of Zemoria

The Fracture happened hundreds of thousands of years before Kaelen and Ruun were born. No living being in the main timeline has any memory of before it. The current year in the main story is 7,223 Post-Fracture.

Prehistoric — The Age of Forces

The Six Forces clash eternally. Their friction crystallizes into the First Seed. The World Tree grows. Time is fluid, not yet linear. No civilization. No gods. Only the raw machinery of existence.

Prehistoric — The Golden Age of Gods and Men

The Rootborn appear across Zemoria as adult superhuman mortals — no memories, no origin. They do extraordinary things and slowly become heroes. Civilizations flourish alongside them at a level of advancement never seen again. The Tree is young and strong. Magic is abundant. Each Rootborn eventually receives a Rootforged weapon from the World Tree.

Prehistoric — The Faithless War

Vhalgor, the god of death, is corrupted by the Shadowflame Tree. His guilt becomes self-hate, his self-hate becomes hate for the world, and his hate for the world becomes pure rage. All eleven other Rootborn attempt to reach him — and fail. He writes the Last Testament as a vessel for his own survival after death. The gods clash. All twelve die. Advanced civilization collapses entirely. The Keepers are established to guard the twelve Rootforged weapons.

Year 0 PF — The Fracture

The World Tree's filtering system fails catastrophically from damage sustained in the Faithless War. Magic becomes unstable. Reality warps in Fracture-zones. The Unremembered appear — beings and memories that can no longer exist in the damaged balance. Time becomes linear for the first time.

Years 1–7,223 PF — The Long Dark

Civilization slowly rebuilds from ruins far greater than anyone understands. The Grove establishes the Age of Silence — selectively forgetting dangerous truths to preserve stability. Kaelen and Ruun rise. The Last Testament surfaces and finds its intended vessel.

Year 7,223 PF — The Events of the Current Books

The village of Glaire vanishes. The seal on the Underworld cracks. Ilan becomes Ruun's vessel. The siege of the World Tree Palace. Kaelen falls. The World Tree burns. Elira escapes with the infant Kaerion, then dies shortly after leaving him with a foster mother. Ruun claims the throne of the world.

Post 7,223 PF — The Future (The Living Legend)

Kaelen's son Kaerion rises. Both Trees fall. The Six Forces are reunited for the first time since before the Fracture. Power is shared among three: Kaerion, Brask, and Serai. A small anomaly in time is noticed in the epilogue — something intervened.

Vhalgor — The Shadow That Survived

Previously known as Voren the Stoneheart. God of death, guardian of the underworld, author of the Last Testament, and the only Rootborn to survive the Faithless War.

This entire section contains significant spoilers for The Lord Under and for the planned book Pantheon.
Forces: Order + Destruction Weapon: The Clawed Gauntlet

Domain & Origin

Vhalgor's domain is death, mountains, patience, and erosion. He is the guardian of the Underworld. He did not choose this domain — it was given to him by a single act and the story people told about it afterward. Once the story was told enough times, it became true.

The First Kill — How His Domain Was Chosen

Vhalgor was the first Rootborn to kill someone during his heroic acts rather than simply neutralizing the danger. Every other god had found ways to protect without ending a life. People saw what he did. The story they told was: he is the one who decides who lives and who dies. That story, told enough times, became his reality. His domain expanded without his consent. The other gods kept their distance — not out of cruelty, but because death makes even immortals uneasy. He had more worshippers than almost anyone. He was more alone than all of them. That isolation is the crack the Shadowflame Tree found.

The Five Stages of His Fall

Stage 1 — Guilt: I did something the others haven't. I crossed a line they haven't. I am different now.
Stage 2 — Self-Hate: I am not just different. I am lesser. Everything they try to give me I don't deserve. All eleven others tried to reach him at this stage. He could not receive their love. It felt like pity.
Stage 3 — Hate for the World: The world made me this. The world owes me something it can never pay back. Pity confirmed he was broken.
Stage 4 — Pure Rage: No more reasoning. No more grief. Just the fire left when everything else burns away. The Shadowflame Tree whispered: yes.
Stage 5 — The Vessel: The Shadowflame Tree found its opening in the rage. Vhalgor wrote the Last Testament — not as a manual but as a trap. A vessel for himself and the Tree's influence to survive his body's destruction and find a new host. He is the only Rootborn to survive the Faithless War.

The Last Testament

Not a book — a predatory idea given form. Its first teaching — "the World Tree is a prisoner, not a protector" — is actually true. This is what makes it uniquely dangerous: genuine truth used as bait for everything that follows. It does not create disciples. It reassembles Vhalgor through the suffering of others.

Through its most successful vessel, Ruun, Vhalgor is technically the sole survivor of the Faithless War — steering from inside, using Ruun's genuine pain as the wheel.

Not a god — a presence. Born from the absence where Forces failed to coalesce. The silence that remained after the Fracture. Worshipped by no one, remembered by few. Central to the deepest mysteries of Zemoria.

What Makes Gods Different From Each Other

Six of the twelve share Force combinations in pairs: Thuramos and Helwár (Chaos + Will), Gaelara and Elara (Creation + Time), Tainos and Vhalgor (Order + Destruction). What makes them distinct beings despite identical Forces is entirely their worshippers — the stories told, the needs projected, the prayers spoken. Identity shaped by expectation, even for gods. This is the philosophical heart of the planned book Pantheon.

Rootforged Weapons

A gift from the World Tree, forged from the Root of each individual god at the height of their power. No known mortal has survived the forging process unaided. Each weapon's form is determined by the god's deepest desire — not by their worshippers.

First Contact Rule: A Rootforged weapon temporarily floods the mind of a mortal who picks it up for the first time — the god's knowledge, memories, and understanding of the Forces all at once. Those with strong enough souls survive this and emerge with knowledge they have no natural way of possessing. This is the source of the sudden, unexplained leaps in civilization — in architecture, astronomy, agriculture, and philosophy — that historians still cannot fully explain.
Prophet Rule: People descended from Rootborn gods carry diluted divine blood. Near residual Force energy or weapon sites, this blood activates as visions — not magic, but resonance. These people, throughout human history, were called prophets, oracles, seers, and shamans.
Thuramos — Stormcleave (war hammer)

Head carved from the first mountain he ever split, veined with crystallized lightning frozen inside permanently. His deepest desire: to never be the thing that failed when everything depended on him. Never misses. Always returns. Makes no sound until impact — the impact sound is thunder.

Solaris — Dawnpiercer (golden bow)

His deepest desire was to illuminate — to reveal, to burn away what was false from a distance. Arrows of condensed light that don't wound conventionally — they reveal truth. Strike a lie and it unravels. Strike a person and they see themselves clearly for the first time. Devastating in battle because most things cannot withstand being truly seen.

Gaelara — Rootspire (living spear)

Never cut from anything — it grew. A single branch of the World Tree itself, still alive, its tip hardening into whatever material the earth beneath her feet is made of. Her deepest desire: that nothing be truly lost, that every fallen thing find its way back into the cycle of growth and decay.

Tainos — Threadcutter (great scythe)

His deepest desire: for everything to find its proper ending — the final note of a song played correctly. The blade is made from silence itself. It makes no sound when it moves. Things cut by it feel only completion. It cuts what is ready to be cut and leaves what isn't.

Nyris — Abysscall (trident)

Three tines of different lengths — the ocean is never level. Made from the bones of the Leviathan's first shedding. His deepest desire: not to conquer the sea but to be understood by it, to have his grief witnessed. When struck against the ocean floor, produces a sound so low it can only be felt. Creatures of the deep surface not in aggression but in mourning.

Syritha — Galewhirl (twin blades)

Two curved blades joined at the hilt, designed to be spun rather than swung — the weapon fights like a dance. Made from compressed storm wind, solid to the touch but visually translucent. Cannot be used by someone fighting from fear or duty. The moment a wielder calculates, the blades lose their edge entirely.

Aphiryn — Fatebind (needle and thread)

A needle the length of a short sword, made from the light of a star that died before Zemoria existed. The thread is invisible, infinite, drawn from Songroot itself. In battle it stitches — connecting cause to consequence, making the threads of causality visible to anyone nearby. Enemies find themselves unable to act without seeing exactly where that action leads.

Helwár — Galecaller (hollow spear)

The shaft is hollow and wind passes through it constantly, producing a low note that changes pitch with speed. In battle it sounds like a living thing. Beasts of any kind will not attack the wielder — not out of fear but recognition. Something ancient in animal instinct says: this is something older than predator and prey.

Morian — Mercykill (curved dagger)

Deliberately small — because Morian understood that the most merciful ending is a quiet one. Always slightly cool to the touch. Causes no pain whatsoever on contact — things cut by it feel only release. Has no visible edge to the naked eye. It looks blunt. It is not.

Elara — Memorychord (lyre)

The only Rootforged weapon that is purely an instrument. Seven strings, each one a different age of the world before the Fracture. Her deepest desire: for nothing to be forgotten. In the vicinity of Memorychord, things that were forgotten begin to resurface. Playing it near a Fracture zone causes the location itself to replay its history like a living recording.

Vhalgor — The Clawed Gauntlet

Before corruption: an open palm — what he wanted most was to hold the world safe in his hands. After corruption: the palm grew claws, roots extending from his hand drawing from his own Root, capable of building any weapon he could envision. Permanently attached to whichever hand it formed on. The corruption didn't change the desire — it weaponized it. He still wanted to hold the world. Now he tears it apart to do it.

Lyria — Firstflame (eternal torch)

Her deepest desire: for things to begin — for the first step to always be possible no matter how dark or how lost. The torch never goes out. Cannot be extinguished by wind, water, or time. In the presence of Firstflame, people find themselves suddenly capable of beginning things they had convinced themselves were impossible. The weapon most responsive to mortal wielders, because Lyria's domain was always about choice — and mortals make choices in the face of genuine uncertainty.

Three Weapons Still Undiscovered

  • Aphiryn's Fatebind — buried near the Nazca plateau in Peru. The Nazca people built their famous lines as a conduit system from a secondhand divine download — the weapon itself was never found by them.
  • Tainos's Threadcutter — buried beneath the unexcavated sections of Göbekli Tepe in Turkey. Only a fraction of that site has been excavated. Something is in the sections archaeology has not yet reached.
  • Morian's Mercykill — somewhere in Japan, in a location considered too sacred to excavate.

Zemoria & Earth — A Hidden History

After the Keeper bloodline ends, the timelines of Zemoria and Earth slowly merge. The myths of ancient civilizations are the degraded oral traditions of Zemoria's real history. The gods they worshipped were real. Their divine weapons were real.

Mortal civilizations who found a Rootforged weapon could not simply wield it — the process that produces them requires a god's Root. Instead, across generations, these civilizations reverse-engineered the World Tree's conductor function using stone and geometry and sacred scale. They couldn't grow a Tree. So they built one. Out of rock. Their great structures are not primarily tombs or astronomical calendars — they are Force channeling devices.

Caral-Supe — The Most Significant Discovery

The oldest city in the Americas (3000–1800 BCE), contemporaneous with the earliest Egyptian pyramids. Six pyramids built around music — flutes from pelican and condor bones, cornets from llama and deer bones found inside the pyramids themselves. No weapons. No battlements. No evidence of warfare anywhere at the site. A civilization of remarkable complexity built entirely without violence.

Their pyramids are acoustic resonance structures. Their sunken circular plazas are acoustic chambers. The entire city of Caral is a conduit built to amplify Elara's Memorychord — to make the music of memory louder, to push it further across time. When their resources shifted, they simply left, carrying their knowledge forward into Andean civilization — seeding the Maya's calendrical mastery generations later.

Key Characters

The people who shaped — or will shape — Zemoria across its stories. Character entries marked with spoiler warnings contain major plot information.

KA

Kaelen

God-King of Zemoria

Root Type: Starhaven (Order + Time) — Rank IX
Born in the last days before the Fracture, Kaelen is one of the few beings with any echo of what came before. He ruled Zemoria for six thousand years, maintaining the Age of Silence — the belief that some truths are too dangerous to remember. His relationship with Ruun is the emotional axis of the series: he loved him genuinely and erased him systematically, and both things are true.

Kaelen falls to Ruun during the siege of the World Tree Palace. His dying act is entrusting his son Kaerion to Elira. In death, he transforms into the Force of Order — an eternal presence rather than a person. His final words to Ruun: "I tried… I stayed."

RU

Ruun

The Lord Under

Root Type: Originally Songroot (Time + Creation), corrupted to Shadowflame — Rank VIII via the Last Testament
Kaelen's brother-in-bond and co-victor against the Leviathan. Systematically erased from history over six thousand years — his portrait removed from the Wall of Heroes, his victories attributed to Kaelen alone. This erasure was not just an insult but a literal halving of his divine power. His corruption was genuine pain exploited by Vhalgor's influence through the Last Testament.

Ruun claims the throne after Kaelen's fall. His reign destabilizes reality itself as the Shadowflame Tree corrupts the world. In the Living Legend timeline, Kaelen's son Kaerion eventually allows Ruun's transformation rather than simply destroying him — and Ruun becomes the Force of Destruction. His final words: "You should have stopped me."

IL

Ilan

The Shadow-Eater

Born in Glaire under a bad sign. Her second shadow appeared when she was six and had developed opinions of its own by twelve. A cursed girl from a forgotten village who became the centre of something far larger than herself.

Ilan becomes Ruun's chosen vessel, her hunger for belonging weaponized against her. She is consumed by Ruun at The Lord Under's climax — her heart devoured, her body torn apart. But her discarded humanity, the remnant Ruun couldn't use, fertilizes Kaelen's seed and becomes the unexpected seed of hope in the ruins of everything. An accidental hero who never knew she was one.

EL

Elira

Kaelen's Royal Consort

Kaelen's royal consort and closest companion from the very beginning of his journey. Her flame affinity comes from ancient heritage predating the World Tree itself — her family carried a direct soul-connection to the Forces of Creation and Chaos, which in combination produces flame: older and more volatile than Solaris's light.

Elira carries the infant Kaerion from the burning World Tree Palace and leaves him with a foster mother before dying shortly after. She never learns what he becomes. She doesn't get closure. She simply does the last thing she can do for the man she loved and then she's gone — one of the series' most quietly devastating endings.

KR

Kaerion

The Living Legend (In Writing)

Root Type: Pure Will — Rank: Beyond X
Kaelen's son. Raised in the slums after Elira left him with a foster mother. His foster mother was executed publicly by Ruun in Kaerion's early twenties. He becomes a protector-thief who believes himself purely human — until the palace crown reacts to his presence. The confluence of his father's Starhaven bloodline and his mother's ancient Force-connection explains why he can access something no one in Zemoria's history has accessed before: a direct, unmediated link to the Force of Will.

??

The Keeper

Songroot protagonist — name yet to be revealed

A young woman in the medieval era who does not believe in magic — like practically everyone else in her world. She awakens the Keeper powers of her great-grandmother: the ability to touch Songroot, the hidden layer of reality where time and causality intertwine, and make small precise adjustments to the flow of events. She didn't ask for this. She doesn't want it. And she navigates it entirely alone in a world that would not believe her if she tried to explain.

She dies accidentally — slips and breaks her neck after going out for drinks. The most mundane possible exit for someone who carried the weight of all of time. Her death ends the Keeper bloodline entirely, freeing destiny to run unconstrained. The two timelines of Zemoria and Earth, no longer held separate by the Keepers' interventions, slowly become seamless. The myths were real. The weapons are out there.

The Zemoria Chronicles — Series Structure

All books in the series, their place in the timeline, and what layer of the world's lore they explore.

Pantheon

Planned

The origin epic. Twelve extraordinary strangers appear across Zemoria as adult superhumans with no memories and no origin. They become heroes. They become gods. The Golden Age of civilization flourishes alongside them. Then Vhalgor falls. The Faithless War ends everything.

Format: A transcript of an amalgamation of oral stories, songs, and poems — assembled like ancient history recovered through fragments. The narrative begins as a chorus of many voices. Slowly, Vhalgor's chapters grow longer. The others grow shorter. By the final act, almost everything is Vhalgor — not because he is the only one worth remembering, but because the others hurt too much to remember clearly.

Timeline: Prehistoric — hundreds of thousands of years before the main series.

Songroot

Planned

Set in an alternate timeline — a medieval era where magic has been forgotten and the gods are bedtime stories. A young woman who doesn't believe in magic discovers she has inherited a duty that has been passed through her bloodline for thousands of years. She didn't ask for it. She doesn't want it. And the weight of all of history rests on whether she can bear it.

Lore layer: What happens when the right ending never comes. The cost of rage over reason.

Future Books — The Weapons in Human History

Future

Stories set across various points in Earth's history and potentially the present day or near future — following the discovery and wielding of Rootforged weapons by human civilizations across the ages. The myths were real. The weapons are still out there. Three remain undiscovered.
